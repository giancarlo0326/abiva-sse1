Identifying Cause and Effect

When you read a story, you can think about cause and effect.

The cause tells why something happens in the story.

The effect tells what happens because of it.

For example, if a character forgets an umbrella (cause), the character gets wet (effect).

Looking for cause and effect helps you understand the story better and see how events are connected.

VOCABULARY

Words That Describe Nonliving Things, Landforms, and Bodies of Water

Special words are used to describe things around us. These are called content-specific words. They help us name and describe nonliving things, landforms, and bodies of water.

Nonliving things are things that do not grow, breathe, or move on their own.

Examples: a table, a pencil, a rock, a car

You can describe them using words like:

  • hard (The rock is hard.)
  • soft (The pillow is soft.)
  • bright (The lamp is bright.)
  • small (The coin is small.)

Landforms are the shapes of the land.

Examples: mountain, hill, valley, plain

You can describe landforms using words like:

  • high (The mountain is high.)
  • low (The hill is low.)
  • wide (The plain is wide.)

Bodies of water are places where water collects.

Examples: river, lake, ocean, pond

You can describe them using words like:

  • deep (The ocean is deep.)
  • shallow (The pond is shallow.)
  • long (The river is long.)

These content-specific words help us tell more about things we see in the world.

GRAMMAR

Using Naming Words

Words that name people, places, animals, days or things are called naming words. A naming word tells who or what something is.

You can use naming words when you talk, read, or write. A person, a place, an animal, and a thing all have names. Examples of naming words are girl, school, dog, and book. Knowing naming words helps you understand sentences better.
