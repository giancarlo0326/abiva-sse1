Nanay’s Magical Meals
Thelma Grace Abiva-Leung
(Adapted from “Mama What’s for Lunch” by Sally Partridge)

Kiko and Nena were playing piko in the yard.
Suddenly, a voice rang out from the house.
“Kikooooo! Nenaaaaa! Time for lunch!” called Nanay.
“Uh-oh, time to eat!” Kiko told their friends.
“Let’s go before the food gets cold—or eaten by Tatay!”
Kiko and Nena ran home fast.
Because every lunch with Nanay is always a surprise!

Where does Kiko and Nena go during lunch time?
What do you think will Sunday’s lunch look like?

On Sunday, Nanay made a bangus with rainbow colors! A special treat! “Splash splash!” said Kiko. But it was just haluhalo— cold and sweet!

On Monday, Nanay made a tarsier with big round eyes!“BOO! It’s staring at me!” cried Kiko, hiding behind his spoon.

But it was only meaty Pinoy spaghetti—with cheesy eyes and hotdog ears!

On Tuesday, Nanay served a butterfly with chicken wings. “TIKTILAAAAOK!” Nena laughed. But really, it was warm tinola with chicken, sayote, and malunggay leaves.

On Wednesday, Nanay served wiggly caterpillars for lunch! “EEEEEEK! Creepy crawlies!” Nena shrieked. But they were just yummy longganisa.

On Thursday, Nanay made a jeepney from bread! “VROOOOM! VROOOOM!” shouted the kids, covering their ears. But it was just a soft, sweet pandesal with strawberry jam.

On Friday, Nanay turned the table into a garden! There were mango flowers, berry birds, and flan petals. “TWEET TWEET!” chirped Nena. It was a treat of creamy leche flan—her favorite!

Then, on Saturday, “Nanay, what’s for lunch today?” asked Nena, peeking into the kitchen.

“Yes, Nanay!” said Kiko. “What magical food are you making now?”

Their noses twitched. Their tummies rumbled. Their eyes sparkled. Nanay smiled and opened the pot.

“Tadaaa!”

It wasn’t shaped like a fish or a bug or a butterfly. It wasn’t even silly.

It was just them—Tatay, Nanay, Kiko, and Nena—sitting together at the table.

And what was for lunch? Chicken adobo with garlic rice. Simple. Warm. Delicious.

“Yummmmm!” they all cheered. “Our favorite! Thank you, Nanay!” And they ate every bite together.

Relating to the Story

Stories are like magic mirrors. You see characters doing the same activities you do, such as celebrating birthdays or having meals with their family. Sometimes, they even feel the same way you do.

They get excited for a party or get sad for a broken toy.

When you think about your own experience or feeling that was similar to something in a story, it means you are relating to it. Just like Kiko and Nena in the story, have you ever been excited about a food prepared by your mother? Have you ever tried eating a fun and creative lunch?

The next time you listen to or read a story, think about these questions:

  • Did something in the story remind you of yourself?
  • Did it remind you of something you have done?
  • How did the characters feel?
  • Did you ever feel the same way?

Stories can be even more fun when you connect them to your own experiences.
