Pipo: What? How do you know?
Mama: In the Philippines, we have heroes called bayani. They did not have superpowers, but they fought and worked hard to help others and protect our country.
Pipo: Wow! Who are they, Mama? Tell me more!
Mama: Okay! Let’s start with our national hero, Jose Rizal. He did not fight with weapons, but he wrote books like Noli Me Tangere and El Filibusterismo. His words inspired Filipinos to stand up against the Spaniards so we could be free.
Pipo: Jose Rizal is awesome! Tell me more, Mama!
Mama: Next, we have Andres Bonifacio. He is called the Father of the Philippine Revolution. He started the Katipunan, a group of brave Filipinos who fought for independence from Spain.
Pipo: Wow! Andres Bonifacio was so brave!
Mama: There is also Melchora Aquino, known as the Mother of the Katipunan. She helped the revolutionaries by giving them food, medicine, and a safe place to rest.
Pipo: She sounds so kind and caring! I want to learn more about these heroes!
Mama: Great idea, Pipo! Tomorrow, we’ll visit the bookstore and find a book about Filipino heroes. How does that sound?
Pipo: Yes, Mama! I’d love that!
Pipo: Mama, Jose Rizal, Andres Bonifacio, and Melchora Aquino lived a long time ago. Can people still be heroes today?

What did Pipo ask Mama about? What did Mama say about modern-day heroes?

Mama: Of course! We call them modern-day heroes. Let us use the computer and look for some examples.
Mama: Here is one: Efren Peñaflorida. He is a teacher who started the Kariton Klasroom. It is a pushcart full of books and supplies, so he can teach poor children who can’t go to school.
Pipo: That is amazing! Who else, Mama?
Mama: Look! Here is Hidilyn Diaz. She is the first Filipino to win an Olympic gold medal in weightlifting. Her hard work inspired so many people!
Pipo: She must be so strong! Is there another hero?
Mama: Yes! Meet AG Saño, also known as Whale Boy. He is an artist who paints murals of dolphins to teach people about saving the environment and fighting climate change.
Pipo: They all sound so cool! Filipinos must be really proud of them!
Mama: Yes, we are! Heroes do not need capes or superpowers, Pipo. They just need kind hearts and the courage to help others.
Pipo: Then I will be like them! I will be a real superhero!
Mama: You already are, anak.

Using Describing Words to Describe Patterns in Community

People in a community use math every day. Helpers, workers, and even community heroes notice patterns to do their jobs well. Following routines and working together often involves patterns, so that everyone stays safe and happy.

Patterns

A pattern is something that repeats. We can see patterns in our community. Some examples of patterns are the following:

  • the colors of jeepneys lined up in order
  • tiles on the sidewalk
  • flags used during town events
  • the order of helpers in a community cleanup
  • how our community heroes act on something good for the neighborhood

A repeating pattern shows the same colors, shapes, or actions repeatedly. Helpers and heroes follow these patterns every day to make the community run smoothly. When everyone works together and follows routines, the community becomes safe, fun, and organized.

GRAMMAR

How Language Shows Our Culture

Did you know that many places in the Philippines have names that come from our own languages? These names often tell us something about:

  • what the place looks like
  • what plants or animals can be found there
  • important people in our culture
  • things that are special to Filipino life

Example:

  • The word ilog means river, so a place near a river might be called Ilog Street.
  • Kawayan means bamboo, so a street with many bamboo plants may be called Kawayan Road.
  • Some streets or places are named after heroes, like Mabini Street, to show respect for our history.
  • Other places are named after flowers, like Rosas Street or Sampaguita Lane, because these plants grow in our country.

Every name has a story. When you read or hear these names, you learn something about the language, traditions, and culture of the Filipino people.
