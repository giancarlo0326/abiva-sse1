SPEAKING

Communicating One’s Needs

Sometimes, you may need help or want something while you are at school. You could be asking for water, needing to go to the bathroom, or asking for help with your schoolwork. It is important to use words to tell others what you need instead of staying quiet or getting upset.

Here are some examples of how you can ask for something politely. Read the sentences.

  1. If you are thirsty, you can say,
    "I am thirsty. Can I drink from the water fountain?"
  2. If you need to go to the restroom, you can say,
    "May I go to the bathroom, please?"
  3. If you need a pencil, you can say,
    "May I please borrow a pencil?"
  4. If you need help with your schoolwork, you can say,
    "Can you help me, please?"
  5. If you need to share something, you can say,
    "May I use the crayons after you?"

Everyone has needs, and it is okay to ask for help. You can use the phrases “May I” and “Can I” to tell others what you need or what you want to do. Speaking clearly helps others understand you better.

How do you help in making sure that your classroom is clean? Do you help your teachers and classmates clean your classroom? What do you think will happen if you do not help in cleaning?

Listen and get to know a pupil who does not help in cleaning their classroom.

Cleanup Day at School
Thelma Grace Abiva-Leung

Lily and Teacher Mia were sitting on the reading rug. The class had just finished cleaning the room together.

Nina yawned and said, “I feel sleepy, Teacher! I worked so hard today.”

Lily smiled and added, “I love Fridays! I had so much fun during our Cleanup Time!”

Teacher Mia asked, “What was the best part of your day, Lily?”

Lily thought for a moment. “It was when we were cleaning the classroom, and you played music on the speaker. You started singing while arranging the books. Jamal was wiping the tables and began dancing! His moves were so funny! Soon, everybody was singing and dancing while putting the crayons away. I even swept the floor while twirling like a ballerina. It was so much fun helping together.”

Teacher Mia laughed and said, “You are right! It feels great to work as a team and keep our classroom neat.”

But Lily remembered that a few months ago, she did not like Cleanup Time at all.

She used to hide her broom or pretend she still had work in her notebook. Sometimes she said, “My tummy hurts,” just to get out of helping!

Teacher Mia would gently remind her, “In our class, everyone has a job. When we all help, our room stays clean and safe.”
But Lily did not listen.

One Friday, Teacher Mia let Lily skip her cleanup duties. She was happy because she could draw instead of cleaning.

After a few days, she noticed that scraps of paper were all over the floor. Some crayons were broken, and the books were out of place. The classroom looked messy! Ben almost tripped on a toy, and Nina sneezed because of the dust.

What do you think Lily feels after seeing the messy classroom? If you were Lily, what would you feel? What will you do?

Lily felt sad. She realized the classroom was untidy because she did not help. Everyone else was trying—Ben stacked the chairs, Nina sorted the pencils, and Teacher Mia kept wiping tables. Lily was the only one not helping.

She started to cry. Teacher Mia knelt beside her and asked, “What is wrong, Lily?”

“I am sorry, Teacher,” Lily said softly. “Now I understand. When I do not help, our classroom becomes messy, and it makes things harder for everyone. We all need to do our part.”

Teacher Mia smiled and hugged her. “I am glad you understand, Lily. We really need your help. Next Cleanup Day, let us work together!”

From then on, Lily joined Cleanup Time with a happy heart. She never missed it again.

Before heading home, Lily waved at her teacher and said, “Goodbye, Teacher Mia! Today was a good day!”

Thelma Grace Abiva-Leung

Last week, our class had a special activity called Cleanup Day. We all worked together to make our playground neat and safe. I wore my gloves and helped pick up trash like paper and wrappers.
