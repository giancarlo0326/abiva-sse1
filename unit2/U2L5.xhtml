Changing Middle Sounds to Make New Words

Words are made up of different letter sounds. The middle sound is the vowel sound in the middle of the word.

The New Student
Amy R. Pomoy

Mona is a new student in our class. She used to go to another school, but her parents decided to transfer her here. She is quiet and seldom joins us during recess. Once, I saw her crying in one corner, but when I approached her, she quickly wiped away her tears.

I told our teacher about seeing her cry, and all she said was, “Mona is still getting used to being in a new school, Tina. We have to be patient with her.”

I also told Nanay about Mona. She patted my head and gently told me to try to be friends with her.

Who was the new student? Why do you think was she crying? Will she find a new friend?

I did try. During recess today, I sat beside her. Mona did not say anything at all, and only looked at me. Sometimes, I think she could not talk. But then, yesterday, I heard her answer one of the teacher’s questions about the story we read, so I know that she could. We both ate in silence.

Later in the afternoon, I shared the swing with her in the playground. I knew she wanted to play, but all the other swings were being used. When I called her name and gave her the ropes, she slowly approached and took them. She still did not say anything, but I could see a faint smile on her lips.

I told Nanay about recess and the swing. She gently combed my hair and told me that I was doing a good job.

The next day, I was surprised to see a small sandwich bag on my table. Inside was a pack of biscuits that I like. I instantly knew it was from Mona. Those were the same kind of biscuits that I ate during recess yesterday. I looked for her in the room and gave her a wave. She smiled back.

Later in the day, I saw Mona waiting for her school bus on one of the benches outside. I sat beside her. Then she started talking, “I did not have any friends in my old school. The children like to call me Monay because they say I am fat. When I cry, they laugh at me.”

I told Nanay about what Mona shared. She hugged me tightly and said, “Some children can be mean and cruel to other children. I am glad that you are not like that. You are lucky to have Mona as your friend. You can show her how children should treat other children.”

What did Tina do for Mona? Did she succeed in making Mona feel comfortable? What did Mona tell her about her experience in her old school? Will Mona and Tina remain friends?

The next day, I invited another classmate, Becca, to eat with me and Mona. At first, Mona was silent again, but she stayed with us the whole time anyway. Later, Becca told me she would gladly spend time with us again.

Every day, the three of us would eat together during recess. On other days, we play in the playground after class. Day by day, Mona became more comfortable having us around.

Today, when the teacher asked who wanted to volunteer to share something they are thankful for, Mona raised her hand and volunteered. “I am thankful for my new friends, Becca and Tina. I am thankful that they spend time with me and treat me nicely.” She said this and looked at us with a smile. I could not be happier!

Using Time Words to Relate Ideas

Time words help connect our ideas and thoughts. They show how one idea leads to another, not just what comes first, but how things happen over time.

Do you still remember what Mona experienced as a new student in the class? Read the sentences below.

First, Mona felt shy and alone at school.

Later, she found friends and began to join them at school.

Finally, she felt happy and belonged.

The words first, later, and finally are time words. You may also use other time words like then, next, and after. These words can be used not only to tell events in a story, but also to relate your own ideas and experiences. Using time words helps others understand your story clearly.

My New Classmate
Anonymous

We have a new student in our class. Her name is Mia. She came from another town. At first, she was quiet and shy. She did not know many people.
