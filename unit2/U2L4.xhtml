SPEAKING

Offer and Clarify Information

Offering information is giving knowledge, details, or any important information about a certain topic. This can be done during a conversation, reporting, answering questions, or sharing an experience.

Clarifying information means giving more details and an explanation of a certain topic so that it can be clearly understood by other people. It helps remove misunderstanding and confusion. This can be done by answering more questions, giving examples, explaining the meaning of certain words, and explaining everything in more detail.

The New Playground Rule
Thelma Grace Abiva-Leung

It was a sunny morning at Liwanag Elementary School. The bell rang, and all the children ran excitedly to the playground.

“Yay! Time to play tag!” shouted Ben, running toward the swings.

“Wait, Ben,” said Teacher Ana. “Remember, we have a new rule. No running near the swings. Someone might get hurt.”

What is the new rule? Do you think everybody will follow it?

Ben nodded, but when Teacher Ana turned around, he whispered to his friend Mia, “Come on, let us play tag here. Rules are boring!”

Mia frowned. “But Teacher Ana said we cannot. What if something happens?”

Ben laughed and ran fast. Suddenly—thud!—Ben bumped into Carlo, who was about to sit on a swing. Both of them fell to the ground.

“Ouch!” cried Carlo. “That hurt!”

Teacher Ana rushed over. “What happened here?” she asked firmly.

Ben looked down. “I did not follow the rule. I was running near the swings. I am sorry, Carlo.”

Carlo rubbed his arm but smiled. “It is okay, Ben. Just be careful next time.”

Teacher Ana nodded. “Rules are not to stop you from having fun. They are here to keep everyone safe. Can we agree to follow the playground rules from now on?”

What are rules for?

“Yes, Teacher Ana,” the children said together.

Ben raised his hand. “I promise to follow the rules. Next time, I will play tag in the open field instead.”

Teacher Ana smiled. “That is the spirit. When we follow rules, everyone stays safe and happy.”

And from that day on, the children enjoyed their games—while keeping the rules in mind.

Identifying Cause and Effect

Sometimes one thing happens because of another. This is called cause and effect.

  • The cause tells why something happens.
  • The effect tells what happens.

Look at these examples:

  • The boy was hungry (cause), so he ate his sandwich (effect).
  • It rained (cause), so the ground got wet (effect).

When you read a story, you can look for the cause and effect to understand it better.

Recognizing the Parts of a Book

Have you ever been to your school library? What books do you grab and read?

Books have many parts that help us know what they are about.

Listen as your teacher shows the different parts of the book. Read about its parts as a class.

  • The cover page is the front of the book. It shows the title, the author, and a picture.

  • The title page is inside the book. It tells the title, author, and illustrator.

  • The author is the person who wrote the book.

  • The illustrator is the person who drew the pictures.

  • The table of contents helps you find the lessons or stories in the book.

  • The spine is the side part that holds all the pages together.

Follow the Rules in the Playground

There are rules in the playground to help everyone play safely and have fun. I follow these rules every day. I wait for my turn on the slide and share the swing with others. I play gently and use kind words with my friends. When someone falls, I help or call a teacher. Following playground rules keeps everyone safe, happy, and ready to play together. When everyone follows the rules, the playground becomes a fun and friendly place for all.
